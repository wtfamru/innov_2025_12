Countries like Sweden, Germany, Croatia, Denmark, and Luxembourg report relatively high depression rates (close to 10%). Countries like Greece, Malta, and Bulgaria report lower depression rates. Norway's Anomaly: Norway, which is not part of the EU, shows a higher-than-average depression rate but is not labeled with an exact percentage. Insights: Countries with higher depression rates might have better mental health awareness and reporting mechanisms. Countries with low depression rates (e.g., Romania) may have underreporting or cultural differences in acknowledging mental health issues. The EU average of 7.2% indicates a significant portion of the population struggles with chronic depression.

The data on self-reported loneliness among older adults aligns with the socioemotional selectivity theory, which suggests that as people age, they prioritize emotionally meaningful relationships but may also experience social isolation due to life transitions such as retirement, health decline, or the loss of loved ones. The high loneliness rates in countries like Greece (62%) and Israel (48%) indicate that cultural and social support systems play a crucial role in elderly well-being. The disengagement theory also explains this trend, proposing that aging individuals naturally withdraw from social roles, leading to reduced social interactions and increased feelings of loneliness. Meanwhile, countries like Denmark (25%) and Switzerland (26%) report lower loneliness levels, which could be attributed to better social policies, community engagement, and elderly care systems. The biopsychosocial model further suggests that loneliness results from a combination of biological (aging effects on the brain), psychological (mental health and perception of social support), and social factors (availability of family and community support).

The data on hospital discharge rates for in-patients with mental and behavioral disorders can be explained through the biopsychosocial model, which emphasizes that mental health conditions result from a combination of biological, psychological, and social factors. The high discharge rates in countries like Germany and Finland suggest that healthcare systems in these regions have more structured inpatient care for mental disorders, while lower rates in other countries may indicate barriers to access rather than a lower prevalence of mental illnesses. The medical model of mental health also applies, as hospitalization is often necessary for severe conditions like schizophrenia, mood disorders, and substance-related disorders, which require intensive treatment.
